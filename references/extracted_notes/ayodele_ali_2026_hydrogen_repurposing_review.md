## Why This Paper Matters

This paper is hydrogen-focused, so it is not a direct CO2 corrosion or dense-phase CO2 source.

It is still useful because it gives a clear example of how repurposing should be assessed case by case. It also reinforces that pressure, material grade, welds, leakage, pressure cycling and monitoring are important when changing the transported fluid.

## Model-Relevant Points

- Repurposing should be assessed case by case.
- Operating pressure can become a major issue after changing service.
- Higher-strength steels may need major modification for hydrogen service.
- Lower material grades such as X52 or below are discussed as more suitable for hydrogen in the paper.
- Welds and joints need careful assessment.
- Leakage, erosion, pressure cycling and monitoring should be included in screening.
- Distributed fibre-optic sensing is mentioned as a possible monitoring technology.
- The paper gives cost ranges for repurposing versus new-build hydrogen pipelines, but these should not be transferred directly to CO2 without validation.

## How We Should Use It

Use this paper as supporting evidence for the screening philosophy:

- do not approve reuse from public data alone;
- reduce confidence when material grade, weld condition or operating history are unknown;
- include pressure and pressure-cycling warnings;
- include monitoring and leak detection as possible work items;
- keep hydrogen-specific conclusions separate from CO2 conclusions.

## Direct Relevance To Our Project

This paper helps our general repurposing logic, but it should not control the CO2 model.

For our CO2 pipeline model, the most useful transferable lesson is:

> changing the fluid changes the risk profile, so each pipeline needs a new service-specific assessment.

## Limitations

- The paper is about hydrogen, not CCS CO2 transport.
- Hydrogen embrittlement logic should not be copied into CO2 calculations.
- The cost numbers are hydrogen-focused and should not be used as direct CO2 cost validation.
